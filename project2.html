First iteration of Order Page

Critique

At school, our website was critiqued and tested in many ways. For example it was test for: responsiveness, load time, functional without Javascript, and feeling of security for the user.

Feedback on our website was relatively positive, but there was a few critique and we took to improve on our website.

What I learned

I learned more from looking at other classmates’ websites than the feedback my partner and I got from our classmates. We used other classmates’ websites as inspiration to improve on our own.

What the order page looks like going into the critique

Iteration: Creating legitimacy

From the critique, I felt that our order forms was lacking legitimacy. I implemented a process bar to show progress in the order form.

But the form was still lacking. I tried discussing with my partner to try to figure out what was missing.

Progress bar implementation

Analysis

I went through a purchase process on STEAM, a online platform that sells games. From that, I screenshotted and analyzed what made me feel “safe” or what made the purchase process feel legitimate.

I noticed that the form is restricted to a certain sizeand there were fine print throughout the form to inform the user in case they felt insecure about something. They specifically let the user know when they were purchasing.

Steam's Cart

Steam's Payment Page

Results

For 7Trades’ order forms, I put in margins so the form was a set size, and it instantly made the forms look more legitimate.

Last iteration of order page

Take away

I learned from this experience that I should look at exisiting examples for reference and see what the standards. It is important for to make the user feel safe when going through an order forms because without the feeling of safety they may not make the purchase on the website.

To continue iterating on the website I would put in more fine print throughout the order forms to create even more security for the user.
